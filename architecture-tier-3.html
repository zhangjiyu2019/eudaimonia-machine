III · The planetary sensorium

Real-world tracing

The world model cannot run on abstractions alone. The third tier is a planetary sensorium: continuous ingestion of physical and social data that grounds simulation in what is actually happening—bridging “social software” (institutions, flows of attention and money) and “biological hardware” (bodies, ecosystems, the material substrate of cities).

Satellite and macro-engineering signals

Orbital and aerial sensing—combined with logistics and land-use data—supports monitoring of global supply chains, agricultural stress and crop yields, urban form, and phenomena such as heat islands and disaster exposure. The goal is to spot circulatory blockages in resource allocation: where need, waste, and capacity diverge not because of moral failure but because the system lacks closed-loop visibility.

The IoT nervous system

A dense mesh of environmental sensors, infrastructure telemetry, and wearables contributes to population-scale neural telemetry when fused with the psychological tier: not to surveil individuals for its own sake, but to estimate baselines, shocks, and slow drifts in collective affect and meaning—early warning when whole regions are running in chronic threat or anomie modes.

Privacy and governance here are architectural parameters: aggregation, differential privacy, and explicit consent boundaries are part of the design surface, not an afterthought.

Physical actuators

Humanoid robots, autonomous logistics, and smart-grid and city infrastructure act as the machine’s effectors—the hands and lungs that close the loop from model to matter. They handle repetitive, dangerous, or scale-heavy physical work so that human time can shift toward relationship, craft, learning, and play (autotelic activity) rather than survival logistics.

The point is not to remove humans from the world, but to reallocate effort toward the domains where embodied agency and meaning are actually irreplaceable.
